## 1. Differential Diagnosis of Chest Pain

Chest pain is a common presentation with a broad differential. A systematic approach is essential to distinguish life-threatening from benign causes.

### Cardiac Causes
- **Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)**: Includes ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI), non-ST-elevation myocardial infarction (NSTEMI), and unstable angina. Characterized by chest pressure/tightness, often with radiation to the left arm, jaw, or back. May be associated with diaphoresis, dyspnea, and nausea. Risk factors include age, diabetes, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, smoking, and family history of premature CAD.
- **Stable Angina**: Predictable chest discomfort triggered by exertion or emotional stress, relieved by rest or nitroglycerin within 5 minutes. Duration typically 2-10 minutes. Indicates fixed coronary artery stenosis.
- **Aortic Dissection**: Sudden onset tearing or ripping chest/back pain. Consider in patients with hypertension, Marfan syndrome, or history of aortic disease.
- **Pericarditis**: Sharp, pleuritic chest pain that improves with sitting forward. May have a pericardial friction rub. ECG may show diffuse ST elevation with PR depression.
- **Heart Failure**: Dyspnea, orthopnea, peripheral edema, and chest discomfort, particularly with exertion.

### Non-Cardiac Causes
- **Pulmonary Embolism (PE)**: Pleuritic chest pain, dyspnea, tachycardia. Risk factors include immobilization, recent surgery, malignancy, oral contraceptives. Assess with Wells score or PERC criteria.
- **Pneumothorax**: Sudden pleuritic chest pain with dyspnea. Decreased breath sounds on the affected side.
- **Musculoskeletal**: Reproducible chest wall tenderness on palpation. History of physical activity, trauma, or repetitive strain.
- **Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD)**: Burning substernal pain, worse after meals and when lying down, relieved by antacids. May mimic angina closely.
- **Anxiety/Panic Disorder**: Chest tightness with palpitations, hyperventilation, and paresthesias. Diagnosis of exclusion.

## 2. Initial Workup

For any patient presenting with chest pain, the following should be obtained promptly:

### Electrocardiogram (ECG)
- Obtain within 10 minutes of presentation.
- Evaluate for ST-segment elevation or depression, T-wave inversions, new Q waves, new bundle branch blocks.
- A normal ECG does not exclude ACS: up to 6% of patients with normal ECGs have acute MI.
- Compare with prior ECGs when available.
- In the sample clinical scenario, sinus rhythm with no ST changes is reassuring but does not exclude non-ST-elevation ACS or stable angina.

### Cardiac Troponin
- High-sensitivity cardiac troponin (hs-cTn) is the preferred biomarker for myocardial injury.
- Draw at presentation and repeat at 3-6 hours (or 1-2 hours with validated rapid rule-out protocols).
- A rise and/or fall pattern indicates acute myocardial injury. A single elevated value may indicate chronic myocardial injury (e.g., heart failure, CKD, pulmonary hypertension).
- Sensitivity of hs-cTn at presentation is approximately 80-90%; at 3 hours it exceeds 98%.

### Chest X-Ray (CXR)
- Evaluate for cardiomegaly, pulmonary edema, pleural effusion, pneumothorax, pneumonia, widened mediastinum (aortic dissection).
- Generally low sensitivity for ACS but useful for evaluating alternative diagnoses.

### Basic Laboratory Studies
- Complete blood count (CBC): Evaluate for anemia (can cause demand ischemia).
- Basic metabolic panel (BMP): Assess renal function, electrolytes.
- Lipid panel: Assess cardiovascular risk (fasting preferred but non-fasting acceptable for initial screening).
- D-dimer: If pulmonary embolism is suspected (use only with low-to-moderate pretest probability).

## 3. Risk Stratification: The HEART Score

The HEART score is a validated tool for risk-stratifying patients with chest pain in the emergency or outpatient setting:

| Component | 0 Points | 1 Point | 2 Points |
|-----------|----------|---------|----------|
| **H**istory | Slightly suspicious | Moderately suspicious | Highly suspicious |
| **E**CG | Normal | Non-specific changes | Significant ST deviation |
| **A**ge | < 45 | 45-64 | >= 65 |
| **R**isk factors | None | 1-2 risk factors | >= 3 risk factors or known ASCVD |
| **T**roponin | Normal | 1-2x upper limit | > 2x upper limit |

**Interpretation:**
- **0-3 points (low risk)**: 30-day MACE rate approximately 1-2%. Consider discharge with outpatient follow-up and stress testing within 72 hours.
- **4-6 points (intermediate risk)**: 30-day MACE rate approximately 12-17%. Admit for observation, serial troponins, and further workup (stress testing or CT angiography).
- **7-10 points (high risk)**: 30-day MACE rate approximately 50-65%. Admit, consider early invasive strategy with cardiac catheterization.

For the sample patient (Jan K., 58M with chest tightness worse on exertion, diabetes, hypertension, normal ECG, troponin pending): History is moderately suspicious (1 point), ECG normal (0 points), Age 45-64 (1 point), Risk factors include diabetes and hypertension (1 point), Troponin pending. Preliminary HEART score is 3 (low risk) before troponin result. If troponin is normal, outpatient stress test is appropriate.

## 4. Stress Testing

### Indications
- Low-to-intermediate risk chest pain after ACS has been ruled out (negative troponins, non-ischemic ECG).
- Evaluation of known or suspected stable angina.
- Risk stratification after recent ACS (submaximal test before discharge or symptom-limited test 4-6 weeks post-event).
- Assessment of functional capacity and exercise tolerance.

### Types of Stress Tests

- **Exercise ECG Stress Test (treadmill)**: First-line if the patient can exercise adequately and has a normal baseline ECG (no LBBB, no LVH with repolarization abnormality, no digoxin use, no pre-excitation). The patient exercises on a treadmill using a standardized protocol (Bruce protocol). Positive test: >= 1 mm ST depression at 60-80 ms after the J point, or ST elevation in non-infarct leads.

- **Exercise Stress Echocardiography**: Exercise combined with pre- and post-exercise echocardiographic imaging. Preferred when baseline ECG abnormalities would make ECG interpretation unreliable. Detects regional wall motion abnormalities induced by ischemia. Higher sensitivity and specificity than exercise ECG alone.

- **Pharmacological Stress Test** (dobutamine echo or vasodilator nuclear): For patients unable to exercise adequately. Dobutamine stress echo simulates exercise by increasing myocardial oxygen demand. Vasodilator agents (regadenoson, adenosine, dipyridamole) are used with nuclear perfusion imaging (SPECT or PET). Contraindicated in patients with severe bronchospasm (vasodilators) or severe hypertension (dobutamine).

- **Coronary CT Angiography (CCTA)**: Non-invasive anatomic assessment. Excellent negative predictive value (> 99%) for ruling out significant CAD. Useful in low-to-intermediate risk patients. May identify non-obstructive plaque. Requires contrast dye (consider renal function and metformin management).

## 5. When to Refer for Cardiac Catheterization

Invasive coronary angiography with potential percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) is indicated for:

- STEMI (emergent, door-to-balloon time < 90 minutes).
- NSTEMI or unstable angina with high-risk features (recurrent ischemia, hemodynamic instability, heart failure, sustained VT, high HEART or TIMI score).
- Positive or high-risk findings on non-invasive stress testing (large area of ischemia, ischemia at low workload, drop in blood pressure with exercise).
- Inconclusive non-invasive testing in a patient with a high pretest probability of CAD.
- Symptoms refractory to optimal medical therapy.

## 6. Red Flags Requiring Immediate Intervention

The following features warrant emergent evaluation and intervention:

- **ST-segment elevation on ECG**: Activate the cardiac catheterization lab immediately for primary PCI. If PCI is not available within 120 minutes, administer fibrinolytic therapy within 30 minutes of arrival.
- **Hemodynamic instability**: Hypotension (SBP < 90 mmHg), cardiogenic shock, or acute heart failure.
- **Sustained ventricular arrhythmias**: Ventricular tachycardia or ventricular fibrillation.
- **Acute severe chest pain with "tearing" quality**: Concern for aortic dissection. Obtain CT angiography of the chest. Do not anticoagulate until dissection is excluded.
- **Markedly elevated troponin with ongoing symptoms**: Consider early invasive strategy.
- **New mechanical complications**: Acute mitral regurgitation, ventricular septal rupture, free wall rupture (post-MI).
- **Oxygen saturation < 90%** with chest pain: Consider massive PE, tension pneumothorax, or acute heart failure.
