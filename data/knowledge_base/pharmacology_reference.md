## 1. Empagliflozin (SGLT2 Inhibitor)

### Mechanism of Action
Empagliflozin selectively inhibits sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 (SGLT2) in the proximal renal tubule. SGLT2 is responsible for approximately 90% of renal glucose reabsorption. By blocking this transporter, empagliflozin promotes urinary glucose excretion (approximately 60-80 g/day), thereby lowering plasma glucose levels independent of insulin secretion. Secondary effects include osmotic diuresis, natriuresis, and modest blood pressure reduction.

### Indications
- Type 2 diabetes mellitus (as monotherapy or combination therapy).
- Reduction of cardiovascular death in patients with T2DM and established cardiovascular disease (based on EMPA-REG OUTCOME trial).
- Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF), regardless of diabetes status.
- Chronic kidney disease with risk of progression.

### Dosing
- Starting dose: 10 mg once daily in the morning.
- May increase to 25 mg once daily if additional glycemic control is needed.
- No dose adjustment required for hepatic impairment. Avoid initiation if eGFR < 20 mL/min/1.73m2 (for diabetes indication; thresholds may differ for HF/CKD indications).

### Side Effects
- Genital mycotic infections (vulvovaginal candidiasis, balanitis): most common, occurring in 5-10% of patients.
- Urinary tract infections: slightly increased risk.
- Volume depletion / dehydration / hypotension: particularly in elderly patients or those on diuretics.
- Euglycemic diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA): rare but serious. May occur with normal or mildly elevated glucose levels. Risk factors include surgery, fasting, illness, low carbohydrate diets, and excessive alcohol intake. Discontinue 3-4 days before major surgery.
- Fournier gangrene (necrotizing fasciitis of the perineum): very rare but reported as a class effect.
- Acute kidney injury: typically related to volume depletion.

### Monitoring
- Renal function (eGFR) at baseline, periodically during treatment, and before dose adjustments.
- Blood pressure, especially in patients on antihypertensives.
- Signs and symptoms of ketoacidosis (nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, fatigue, dyspnea) even with blood glucose < 250 mg/dL.
- Volume status, particularly in patients on loop diuretics or with reduced oral intake.
- HbA1c every 3-6 months.

## 2. Amiodarone

### Mechanism of Action
Amiodarone is a class III antiarrhythmic agent that primarily blocks potassium channels, prolonging the action potential duration and effective refractory period. It also has class I (sodium channel blockade), class II (beta-adrenergic blockade), and class IV (calcium channel blockade) properties. This multi-channel blockade makes it one of the most effective antiarrhythmic agents but also accounts for its complex pharmacology and extensive side effect profile.

### Indications
- Atrial fibrillation (AF): Rate control and rhythm control (cardioversion and maintenance of sinus rhythm). Particularly useful in patients with structural heart disease or heart failure where other antiarrhythmics are contraindicated.
- Ventricular tachycardia (VT) and ventricular fibrillation (VF): Treatment and prevention. Used in ACLS for shock-refractory VF/VT.
- Other supraventricular tachycardias refractory to first-line agents.

### Dosing
- **Oral loading**: Typically 400-800 mg/day in divided doses for 1-3 weeks, then 200-400 mg/day maintenance.
- **Intravenous loading**: 150 mg over 10 minutes, then 1 mg/min for 6 hours, then 0.5 mg/min for 18 hours. Supplemental boluses of 150 mg for breakthrough arrhythmia.
- **Maintenance**: 100-200 mg/day orally for atrial fibrillation; 200-400 mg/day for ventricular arrhythmias.
- Half-life is extremely long: 40-55 days. Steady state may take months to achieve, and effects persist for weeks to months after discontinuation.

### Side Effects
Amiodarone has a uniquely broad and potentially serious adverse effect profile due to its iodine content and lipophilic tissue accumulation:

- **Thyroid dysfunction** (15-20%): Both hypothyroidism and hyperthyroidism. Amiodarone contains approximately 37% iodine by weight. Check TSH at baseline and every 6 months.
- **Pulmonary toxicity** (1-5%): Potentially fatal. Presents as pneumonitis or pulmonary fibrosis. Symptoms include progressive dyspnea, cough, and fever. Obtain baseline chest X-ray and pulmonary function tests. Monitor with annual CXR and immediate imaging if respiratory symptoms develop.
- **Hepatotoxicity**: Elevated transaminases are common (15-30%). Severe hepatotoxicity is rare but can be fatal. Monitor LFTs at baseline and every 6 months.
- **Corneal microdeposits**: Occur in nearly all patients. Usually asymptomatic but may cause visual halos. Annual ophthalmologic examination recommended.
- **Peripheral neuropathy and tremor**: Dose-related.
- **Skin photosensitivity**: Common. Blue-gray skin discoloration with chronic use.
- **Bradycardia and AV block**: Due to beta-blocking and calcium channel blocking properties.
- **QT prolongation**: Although amiodarone prolongs the QT interval, torsades de pointes is relatively rare compared to other class III agents.

### Monitoring Requirements
- **Baseline**: TSH, LFTs, CXR, PFTs, ECG (QTc), ophthalmologic exam, serum electrolytes.
- **Ongoing**: TSH every 6 months, LFTs every 6 months, CXR annually, PFTs if symptomatic, ECG periodically, ophthalmologic exam annually.
- **Drug interactions**: Extensive (see Drug Interactions Reference). Always review co-medications before initiating amiodarone.

## 3. Metformin

### Mechanism of Action
Metformin is a biguanide that lowers blood glucose through multiple mechanisms: it reduces hepatic glucose production (primary effect), increases insulin sensitivity in peripheral tissues (particularly skeletal muscle), and decreases intestinal absorption of glucose. It does not stimulate insulin secretion and therefore does not cause hypoglycemia when used as monotherapy. It also activates AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK).

### Dosing
- Starting dose: 500 mg once or twice daily with meals, or 850 mg once daily.
- Titrate every 1-2 weeks based on tolerance and glycemic response.
- Usual effective dose: 1500-2000 mg/day in divided doses (e.g., 1000 mg BID).
- Maximum dose: 2550 mg/day (rarely used due to limited additional benefit above 2000 mg).
- Extended-release formulation: Start 500 mg once daily with the evening meal, titrate to max 2000 mg/day.

### Contraindications
- eGFR < 30 mL/min/1.73m2 (contraindicated).
- eGFR 30-45 mL/min/1.73m2: Do not initiate; may continue with dose reduction if eGFR falls into this range on existing therapy.
- Acute or chronic metabolic acidosis, including diabetic ketoacidosis.
- Use caution in conditions predisposing to lactic acidosis: hepatic impairment, excessive alcohol use, acute heart failure, sepsis.
- Temporarily discontinue for iodinated contrast procedures and major surgical procedures.

### Key Points for the Clinical Scenario
A 58-year-old male on metformin 1000 mg BID with a plan for stress testing should have renal function checked before any contrast-enhanced study. If coronary CT angiography (contrast) is considered, metformin should be withheld at the time of the procedure and for 48 hours afterward, with renal function reassessed before restarting.

## 4. Lisinopril

### Mechanism of Action
Lisinopril is an ACE (angiotensin-converting enzyme) inhibitor that blocks the conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II. This results in vasodilation, reduced aldosterone secretion, decreased sodium and water retention, and decreased cardiac preload and afterload. ACE inhibitors also reduce degradation of bradykinin, which contributes to their vasodilatory effect (and the side effect of dry cough).

### Dosing
- **Hypertension**: Start 10 mg once daily. Titrate to target over 2-4 weeks. Usual range: 10-40 mg once daily. Maximum: 80 mg/day (rarely needed).
- **Heart failure**: Start 2.5-5 mg once daily. Target dose: 20-40 mg/day.
- **Post-MI**: Start within 24 hours at 5 mg, then 5 mg at 24 hours, 10 mg at 48 hours, then 10 mg daily.
- Adjust for renal impairment: Start at lower dose (2.5-5 mg) if eGFR < 30.

### Monitoring
- **Blood pressure**: At each visit. For the sample patient (BP 155/95 on lisinopril 10 mg), blood pressure is significantly above target (< 130/80 for a diabetic patient). This requires either dose escalation to 20-40 mg or addition of a second antihypertensive.
- **Serum potassium**: At baseline, within 1-2 weeks of initiation or dose change, then every 6-12 months. Risk of hyperkalemia increases with renal impairment, diabetes, potassium supplements, or potassium-sparing diuretics.
- **Serum creatinine and eGFR**: At baseline, within 1-2 weeks of initiation or dose change. A rise in creatinine of up to 30% is acceptable and expected due to reduced glomerular filtration pressure. A rise > 30% should prompt evaluation for bilateral renal artery stenosis or volume depletion.
- **Symptoms of angioedema**: Swelling of the face, lips, tongue, or throat. More common in patients of African descent. Requires immediate discontinuation and emergency treatment.
- **Cough**: Persistent dry cough occurs in 5-20% of patients. If intolerable, switch to an ARB.
