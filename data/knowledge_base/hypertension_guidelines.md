## 1. Classification of Blood Pressure

Blood pressure categories for adults (based on the average of 2 or more properly measured readings on 2 or more occasions):

| Category | Systolic (mmHg) | | Diastolic (mmHg) |
|----------|-----------------|---|-------------------|
| Normal | < 120 | and | < 80 |
| Elevated | 120-129 | and | < 80 |
| Stage 1 Hypertension | 130-139 | or | 80-89 |
| Stage 2 Hypertension | >= 140 | or | >= 90 |
| Hypertensive Crisis | > 180 | and/or | > 120 |

Proper measurement technique requires the patient to be seated quietly for 5 minutes with back supported, feet on the floor, and arm supported at heart level. An appropriately sized cuff must be used. Ambulatory blood pressure monitoring (ABPM) or home blood pressure monitoring (HBPM) may be used to confirm the diagnosis and rule out white-coat hypertension.

## 2. Treatment Targets

- **General adult population**: Target blood pressure < 130/80 mmHg.
- **Patients with diabetes mellitus**: Target < 130/80 mmHg. Blood pressure control in diabetic patients significantly reduces the risk of cardiovascular events, stroke, and progression of diabetic nephropathy.
- **Patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD)**: Target < 130/80 mmHg, particularly if albuminuria is present.
- **Elderly patients (>= 65 years)**: Target systolic < 130 mmHg if tolerated. Avoid excessive lowering (systolic < 110 mmHg) due to risk of falls, syncope, and end-organ hypoperfusion.
- **Patients with coronary artery disease**: Target < 130/80 mmHg. Avoid diastolic blood pressure < 60 mmHg, as this may compromise coronary perfusion.

## 3. Lifestyle Modifications

Non-pharmacological interventions should be recommended for all patients with elevated blood pressure or hypertension:

- **Dietary changes (DASH diet)**: Emphasize fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and low-fat dairy products. Reduce saturated fat and total fat intake. Expected reduction: approximately 11 mmHg systolic.
- **Sodium restriction**: Limit sodium intake to < 2,300 mg/day, ideally < 1,500 mg/day. Expected reduction: approximately 5-6 mmHg systolic.
- **Physical activity**: At least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise or 75 minutes per week of vigorous-intensity exercise. Expected reduction: approximately 5-8 mmHg systolic.
- **Weight loss**: For overweight or obese patients, target a loss of at least 1 kg body weight. Expected reduction: approximately 1 mmHg per kg lost.
- **Alcohol moderation**: Limit to no more than 2 standard drinks per day for men and 1 for women. Expected reduction: approximately 4 mmHg systolic.
- **Smoking cessation**: While smoking does not directly cause sustained hypertension, it dramatically increases overall cardiovascular risk and causes acute blood pressure elevation.

## 4. First-Line Pharmacological Therapy

Four classes of antihypertensive medications are recommended as first-line therapy:

### ACE Inhibitors (e.g., lisinopril, enalapril, ramipril)
- **Mechanism**: Inhibit angiotensin-converting enzyme, reducing angiotensin II production, leading to vasodilation and decreased aldosterone secretion.
- **Preferred in**: Diabetes with albuminuria, heart failure with reduced ejection fraction, post-myocardial infarction, chronic kidney disease with proteinuria.
- **Side effects**: Dry cough (5-20%), angioedema (rare but serious), hyperkalemia, acute kidney injury (especially with renal artery stenosis).
- **Monitoring**: Serum potassium and creatinine within 1-2 weeks of initiation or dose change, then periodically.
- **Contraindications**: Pregnancy, bilateral renal artery stenosis, history of angioedema with ACE inhibitors.

### ARBs (e.g., losartan, valsartan, irbesartan)
- **Mechanism**: Block angiotensin II at the AT1 receptor.
- **Preferred in**: Same populations as ACE inhibitors; used as an alternative when ACE inhibitor cough is intolerable.
- **Side effects**: Hyperkalemia, acute kidney injury. Lower incidence of cough compared to ACE inhibitors.
- **Note**: Do not combine ACE inhibitors and ARBs (increased risk of hyperkalemia, renal impairment, and hypotension without benefit).

### Calcium Channel Blockers (e.g., amlodipine, nifedipine ER)
- **Mechanism**: Block L-type calcium channels in vascular smooth muscle, causing vasodilation.
- **Preferred in**: Elderly patients, isolated systolic hypertension, patients of African descent.
- **Side effects**: Peripheral edema, headache, flushing, gingival hyperplasia.

### Thiazide/Thiazide-like Diuretics (e.g., chlorthalidone, hydrochlorothiazide, indapamide)
- **Mechanism**: Inhibit sodium reabsorption in the distal convoluted tubule, promoting diuresis and long-term vasodilation.
- **Preferred in**: Elderly patients, isolated systolic hypertension, prevention of recurrent kidney stones (calcium-containing).
- **Side effects**: Hypokalemia, hyperuricemia, hyponatremia, hyperglycemia.

## 5. Combination Therapy

Most patients with Stage 2 hypertension (BP >= 140/90 mmHg) or those more than 20/10 mmHg above target will require two or more antihypertensive agents:

- **Preferred two-drug combinations**: ACE inhibitor or ARB + CCB; ACE inhibitor or ARB + thiazide diuretic; CCB + thiazide diuretic.
- **Avoid**: ACE inhibitor + ARB (dual RAAS blockade); beta-blocker + non-dihydropyridine CCB (risk of bradycardia and heart block).
- **Three-drug combination**: If blood pressure remains uncontrolled on two drugs, combine ACE inhibitor or ARB + CCB + thiazide diuretic.
- **Resistant hypertension**: Defined as BP above target despite three optimally dosed antihypertensives including a diuretic. Consider adding spironolactone (25-50 mg/day). Evaluate for secondary causes (renal artery stenosis, primary aldosteronism, pheochromocytoma, obstructive sleep apnea).

## 6. Special Populations

### Diabetes Mellitus
- ACE inhibitors or ARBs are first-line due to their renoprotective effects and reduction of albuminuria.
- Target BP < 130/80 mmHg.
- SGLT2 inhibitors (e.g., empagliflozin) provide modest blood pressure reduction (3-5 mmHg systolic) in addition to their glycemic and cardiorenal benefits.
- A patient on lisinopril 10 mg daily with BP 155/95 mmHg requires intensification: increase lisinopril dose (up to 40 mg daily) or add a second agent (CCB or thiazide diuretic).

### Chronic Kidney Disease
- ACE inhibitors or ARBs are preferred, particularly with albuminuria.
- Monitor potassium and creatinine closely. A rise in creatinine of up to 30% after initiation is acceptable and expected.
- Avoid thiazide diuretics if eGFR < 30 (loop diuretics preferred).

### Elderly Patients
- Start with lower doses and titrate slowly.
- Monitor for orthostatic hypotension (check standing BP). Orthostatic hypotension is defined as a drop in systolic BP >= 20 mmHg or diastolic >= 10 mmHg within 3 minutes of standing.
- Be cautious with agents that may cause falls (alpha-blockers, centrally acting agents).

## 7. Monitoring and Follow-Up

- **After initiating or changing therapy**: Follow up within 2-4 weeks to assess response and side effects.
- **Once at target**: Follow up every 3-6 months.
- **Laboratory monitoring**: Serum electrolytes (potassium, sodium), creatinine, and eGFR at baseline, 1-2 weeks after initiation or dose change of RAAS inhibitors or diuretics, then every 6-12 months.
- **Home blood pressure monitoring**: Encourage all patients to monitor BP at home. Average home readings > 135/85 mmHg generally correlate with office readings > 140/90 mmHg.
- **Assess medication adherence**: Non-adherence is the most common cause of apparent treatment failure. Use open-ended questions and consider pill counts or refill records.
